MISSION STATEMENT

Webb's mission is to provide a dynamic learning community that nurtures and inspires our students and alumni to: - Think boldly, mindfully, and creatively. - Act with honor and moral courage. - Lead with distinction. - Serve with a generous spirit.

THE WEBB SCHOOLS ACADEMIC PROGRAM

Our academic program supports the Schools' mission statement by presenting a rigorous, college-preparatory curriculum that encourages students to think independently, learn collaboratively, and communicate effectively. Our academic program fosters thinkers who learn and understand traditional and emerging disciplines, master skills essential to achievement in college and beyond, and embody vital attitudes and qualities of mind including curiosity, love of learning, and a commitment to what is right and true. The classes in the 2026-27 Course Catalog represent an advancement in the schools' curriculum, combining a series of foundational required core courses with a deep and varied range of electives across Webb's five disciplines: humanities (combining English and history), mathematics, science, world languages, and fine arts. The courses designed for freshmen and sophomores are skills-based and cross-disciplinary. Building on the skills students develop during their first two years at Webb, courses for juniors and seniors introduce students to college-level advanced study and research. A selection of courses at Webb are more rigorous and thus carry an Honors, or Advanced Studies (AdvSt), designation. Honors courses differ from standard sections of a course by incorporating depth and advanced content. Advanced Studies (AdvSt) courses are advanced courses designed by Webb faculty to engage students in high-level intellectual pursuits and are characterized by their depth of study, their embrace of the latest in academic research, and their attention to making connections among disciplines. Across Webb's curriculum, we emphasize creative thinking and problem solving over rote memorization, instilling skills and habits of mind essential for success in college and beyond. Webb students develop as master learners - knowing how to address problems, ask the right questions, and use a variety of resources to articulate and test their own answers - prepared for the road ahead.

COURSE PLANNING

Each student is encouraged to choose a challenging and varied curriculum based on ability and interests, as well as on academic and career goals. Students should plan their programs for the full school year, with subsequent years of study and college in mind. Returning students consult with their advisor, teachers, and parents while making course selections for the following year. New students should discuss course choices with their parents. The director of studies will review the academic programs and overall loads of individual students. In addition, the dean of college guidance will examine junior and senior course selections to ensure that they meet the requirements of the schools to which students are applying. Each student's advisor, department chairs, and the director of studies are available to answer questions about course selection. All students are required to take five courses each semester. Many students elect to take a sixth course in one or both semesters. Students are not permitted to take seven courses.

COURSE PLACEMENT

The departments evaluate students for consideration to take Honors and Advanced Studies courses based on current performance in the discipline, course and exam grades, mastery of course content, and commitment to learning. Some departments also use a diagnostic instrument to assist in the decision-making process. Advisors are told which students have been recommended to take Honors and Advanced Studies courses as part of the course selection process in SIS. There is an appeals process by which students may apply for consideration to take these courses if they have not received departmental approval. The School reserves the right to limit the number of Honors and Advanced Studies courses that a student may take.

COURSE CHANGES: ADDS, DROPS, AND LEVEL CHANGES

Students are expected to take the courses for which they have registered. Much discussion goes into the course selection process, so requests for subsequent changes must be based on compelling reasons and will be scrutinized. Dropping / Adding Courses: Students may request to drop or add courses only during the first seven academic days of each semester. This gives them ample time to evaluate their overall load against specific course requirements. Students planning to add a course should do so as quickly as possible to avoid missing important course content and to minimize class absences. Students can obtain course change forms from the registrar, who will ensure that the desired change can be made within existing scheduling parameters. To make the requested change, a student must obtain written permission from the advisor, the relevant department chair, and the director of studies. The dean of college guidance and the parent / guardian may also be consulted as needed. Students must attend at least the first class of each semester before submitting a course change request. Students are scheduled by subject, not by teacher or time block, and requests for a particular teacher or a free block will not be granted. Requests are subject to department approval. Seniors are given priority. In some cases, full courses or scheduling conflicts may make it impossible to grant a change. The student must continue attending all scheduled classes until a revised schedule is issued. Courses dropped prior to the specified deadline will not appear on student transcripts. Students who drop courses after the deadline may receive a Withdraw Passing (WP) or Withdraw Failing (WF) on their transcripts. Level Changes: When it becomes apparent that a student is inappropriately placed in a course, the student, teacher, or advisor may request a level change. The same procedure applies to level changes as to general course changes. In the event of a level change, the student's official transcript will show only the course the student is enrolled in at the end of the semester. At the time of the change, the student's grade average in the prior course will transfer to the new course, with a 0.5-point boost added if the prior course was Honors, and a 1.0-point boost added if the prior course was an Advanced Studies course.

SUMMER WORK OR COURSE ACCELERATION

Some returning students may be required to take a summer class for remedial work. However, students are rarely allowed to skip a course in Webb's normal sequence through summer work. Too often, students who try to "accelerate" are not equipped to learn such large volumes of material in such a short time. In addition, the director of studies and the dean of college guidance suggest that enrichment, not acceleration, be the goal of summer programs. Requests for returning students to take a summer course to place into an accelerated course must be made in writing to the appropriate department chair and the director of studies by April 15. New students must receive approval from the director of studies by June 1. If the request is approved, the student must fulfill the requirements set by the department chair and the director of studies before being allowed to accelerate. The terms for such acceleration will be determined in partnership between the director of studies and the academic department chair.

ADVANCED STUDIES COURSES, ADVANCED PLACEMENT COURSES, AND EXAMS

In the fall of 2025, The Webb Schools formally discontinued the offering of Advanced Placement (AP) courses. Consistent with Webb's institutional philosophy, which emphasizes unbounded intellectual inquiry and a comprehensive, experience-centered curriculum, all AP courses were replaced with Advanced Studies courses. As part of this academic shift, the school also determined that AP examinations would no longer be administered on campus. The Webb Schools remain steadfast in their commitment to providing an exceptional educational experience and preparing students for success in the college admissions process. In support of this commitment, students are strongly discouraged from registering for AP examinations at external testing sites. Students who elect to sit for AP examinations off campus will not be excused from any Webb academic or extracurricular obligations.

EXPERIENTIAL LEARNING

The Webb Schools provide opportunities for students to extend their education beyond campus through experiential programs. Webb's trips program offers domestic and international trips during school breaks and in the summer. These trips are designed to help students expand their world views and promote unbounded thinking, global fluency, and global citizenship through field study, cultural exploration, language immersion, and community service. Trips are announced each fall and, unless otherwise specified, are open to the entire Webb community. Webb's bi-annual Unbounded Days program is designed to provide immersive, collaborative, and relevant experiences that connect the classroom to the broader world. During Unbounded Days, students can choose from nearly 30 course offerings, including overnight programs, urban trips, outdoor experiences, and more. Unbounded Days courses are interdisciplinary, experiential, and grounded in the resources available both on Webb's campus and in Southern California. Unbounded Days will be held during the spring of the 2027-28 school year.

COURSE WORK AT THE CLAREMONT COLLEGES

Students who complete all advanced-level course offerings in a given department may seek to enroll in courses at The Claremont Colleges, with approval from the department chair, director of studies, dean of students, the college registrar, and the college professor. The Webb Schools must certify completion of all possible related work before recommending a student to The Colleges. Students are responsible for ensuring that schedules do not conflict with commitments at Webb.

PROGRAMS AWAY FROM WEBB

Several viable and accredited opportunities exist for Webb students to apply themselves to a semester or year-length program at another school in their junior year, including programs abroad. Students interested in this option must write a formal proposal detailing the program. The proposal is reviewed by a committee including the director of studies, assistant head of schools, director of financial planning and operations, dean of college guidance, and director of admission. For institutional planning purposes, applications and petitions must be completed and approved by the date student contracts are due in the spring of the previous year. The petition must include materials describing the nature and purpose of the program, the availability of appropriate coursework, and the program's compatibility with Webb's and, where appropriate, UC requirements for promotion and graduation. Grades and transcripts from another institution will be included with Webb transcript materials sent to colleges, but they will not be calculated into a student's Webb GPA.

INDEPENDENT STUDY

Highly qualified students who wish to undertake serious study in an area not covered by the Schools' curriculum may propose an Independent Study course. To be considered, a student must exhaust all standard course offerings in the area of study and then design and document the features of the proposed program. Independent Study courses must be academic in character, undertaken under the auspices of an academic department, and sponsored and supervised by a member of the faculty. They are typically no more than one-half credit and one semester in length. The Academic Council considers all proposals and maintains a high standard for those approved.

ADDITIONAL ACADEMIC RESOURCES

Blended learning combines in-person instruction with online tools to maximize student learning. In a blended learning course, a student learns at least in part through online delivery of content and instruction with some element of student control over time, place, path, or pace. As a school, Webb is actively exploring the role of blended learning in its curriculum and is committed to integrating technology to support broader educational objectives. Webb has adopted Canvas as a uniform learning management system and a one-to-one laptop program for students and faculty.

FRESHMAN SEMINAR

All freshmen participate in a required seminar class that focuses on topics in human development, adjustment to boarding school community life, and important issues in adolescent physical, social, academic, emotional, and personal growth. Classes are kept small to facilitate open, earnest discussion. In addition, all students are required to attend scheduled speakers and discussions through the Health & Living program. Health & Living is a four-year health program designed to target students' specific developmental milestones throughout high school. Topics rotate year-to-year and are designed to build on information discussed within the context of Freshman Seminar. Students who enter Webb after the ninth grade participate in introductory Health & Living meetings to ensure a base knowledge similar to that of their peers.

FAWCETT LIBRARY

Fawcett Library serves as an academic center for The Webb Schools' dynamic curricula. The library's staff offers support and guidance to students and teachers in their intellectual pursuits and research. Materials can be requested and put on reserve, specialized guides can be created to complement instruction and inquiry, and dialogues can be sparked to sustain study. The library is staffed by qualified educators and has an extensive print collection as well as access to online databases and resources including EBSCOhost, New York Times archives, the Gale Discover Collection, ProQuest, JSTOR, and more. The library also hosts academic events and groups including News & Views and a creative writing workshop.

ACADEMIC SUPPORT

The Webb Schools are committed to providing students with the support they need to achieve success at all levels of the academic program. A student's primary source of assistance and guidance is the classroom teacher. Office Hours: Students can attend faculty office hours, offered four times per week, or schedule appointments to meet individually or in small groups with teachers before school, in a common free period, at lunch, or outside the academic day. These sessions can be used to review course content, improve study skills, prepare for tests, or get help on an assignment. Required Office Hours: Students who are consistently struggling to achieve success in one or more courses may be assigned to a weekly program of required office hours. Students participating in this program must attend scheduled help sessions regularly and submit a signed form each week to their advisor and / or respective dean. Study Periods: Students are expected to use unscheduled periods during the academic day to complete assignments, consult with teachers, organize materials, and study. The maximum number of courses any student can take in a semester is six. Students with a five-course load will have additional study time. Study Hours and Evening Labs: Boarding students are scheduled for evening study hours Sunday through Thursday. During this time, students are expected to complete assignments, prepare for upcoming tests and quizzes, work on labs, projects, and papers, receive extra help, and, if time permits, get ahead in classes. All students are encouraged to take advantage of evening academic labs.

TUTORING

The tutoring policy of The Webb Schools has two core goals: first, to ensure that tutors who work on campus are qualified professionals who communicate with academic faculty and administration to best serve students and support the Schools' goals and values; and second, to ensure the safety and security of the residential community. While most students' academic needs are met through interaction with teachers in the classroom, in office hours, in evening labs, and beyond, there are rare occasions when a student needs additional support in a particular subject or skill. The school therefore maintains a pool of approved and vetted tutors who are cleared to work on campus. Individual music instruction may also take place on campus, and all music teachers must be approved as part of the tutor pool. Tutors are considered independent contractors and are paid directly by families. Webb also offers both individual and group SAT tutoring options to families on campus in collaboration with vetted SAT tutoring companies. Information about these companies and their services is available through the Office of College Guidance. For the full tutoring policy, see the Student Handbook.

CREATING A BALANCED SCHEDULE

Here are some perspectives to keep in mind when creating an appropriate schedule: 1. Current and past performance and course load Each department has its own criteria for admission into advanced courses. Students should consider their overall load, especially if recommended into multiple Honors and / or Advanced Studies courses. Ideally, a capable student's transcript should reflect an incremental increase in difficulty from one year to the next. 2. Non-academic commitments and leadership positions Competitive colleges value rigorous courses and high grades, but they also reward significant leadership, service, and extracurricular involvement. These activities are part of living a full and balanced life at Webb and should be weighed when crafting a schedule. 3. Choose the most challenging academic program that is appropriate The key words are "challenging" and "appropriate." For some students, standard college-preparatory courses offer more than sufficient challenge. Others may need multiple Honors or Advanced Studies courses to be challenged appropriately. Many highly talented students still cannot reasonably manage more than two Honors or Advanced Studies classes. Students should challenge themselves in subjects that genuinely interest them. 4. Consider time available and time required Honors and Advanced Studies courses have tougher homework loads. Department chairs estimate workload with either a 1.0 or 1.25 distinction, which accompanies the course descriptions. The goal is to create a challenging but manageable academic program.

CO-CURRICULAR PROGRAMS IN THE AFTERNOON

The Webb Schools conduct a comprehensive afternoon activities program in the belief that these experiences contribute significantly to the development of character, mutual support, and school spirit. Webb's afternoon program provides students with access to a range of academic, arts, community, and athletic offerings. Students may choose to play an interscholastic sport or participate in activities such as robotics, outdoor activities, dance, athletic training, theater, community service, and museum education. All students participate in the program each season of the year, and Webb requires all students to participate in an interscholastic sport at least one season each year. Most programs meet Monday through Friday from approximately 3:30 to 5:30 p.m. Some programs meet in the evening or morning to accommodate multiple team practices. Athletic and afternoon programs involving competitions may include evening or weekend commitments. Some activities require specialized equipment. Art Studio (offered in spring)

Art Studio allows students to explore their own artistic interests in topic, content, and media with guided support. Students learn how to prepare an independent project, conduct research into methods and techniques, manage a project calendar, and execute a finished piece tailored to their own area of interest. Prior visual arts experience is not required.

Dance Company (offered in fall and winter)

In the fall season, Webb's dance company explores dance as a performing art through observation and practice. Students study how movement informs human experience, with classes that typically include a warmup, across-the-floor progressions, and a center-floor combination. The winter dance program builds off the fall program; fall participation is not required, but new students must audition. The winter dance program counts as a sports credit because of its physical demands and culminating performance.

Debate (offered in fall)

The afternoon debate activity serves as an introduction to the Webb debate program. Through games and activities, students practice argumentation, refutation, plans, counterplans, and other key debate techniques. Many participants choose to continue into tournaments with the Webb Debate Team.

Literary Magazine (offered in spring)

Students create, edit, and publish a literary magazine for the Webb community. As editorial staff, students craft a mission and vision, solicit submissions of poetry, fiction, creative nonfiction, photography, art, and more, and select the best submissions for publication.

Music Performance (offered in winter)

This activity is open to instrumentalists, vocalists, and pianists. Depending on the skill level and instrumentation in the class, students may engage in private practice with coaching, small ensemble practice, jazz combo work, individualized music study, or a self-designed music project.

Musical Theater (offered in spring)

Musical Theater involves rehearsing and producing a full-length musical to be performed in the Liu/Cheung Theater for a three-night run in May. During rehearsals, students work on vocal techniques, dance and movement, script analysis, and song interpretation.

Paleontology Museum Lab (offered in fall, winter, and spring)

The Alf Museum serves the campus, local community, and world as a center for research and education on our planet's history. Student volunteers assist in tasks such as cleaning fossils in the preparation lab, laser scanning dinosaur bones, and maintaining exhibits. No prior experience is required.

Robotics (offered in fall and winter)

Through Webb's affiliation with the FIRST Tech Challenge program, robotics teams design, build, program, and operate robots to compete in head-to-head challenges. Students develop STEM skills, practice engineering principles, build robots from stock materials, and program them in languages including Java. Teams also design and market their brand and perform community outreach.

Science Fair Research Lab (offered in winter)

Science Fair Research Lab provides an environment for students to explore independent scientific research and prepare for science fairs such as the Los Angeles County Science Fair and The Archer School STEM Fair. Students receive hands-on support and mentorship in research design, data generation, and public presentation.

Technical Theater (offered in fall, winter, and spring)

Technical Theater provides hands-on training in the non-performance side of productions. Students explore design research and principles, scene shop organization, painting and construction techniques, sound, lighting, computer technology, special effects, publicity, business management, theater safety, and the function of technical stage personnel in production work.

Theater (offered in fall)

Each fall, the theater program rehearses and produces a full-length play to be performed in the Liu/Cheung Theater for a three-night run in November. Students hone their performance skills through acting techniques, script analysis, and exploratory performance work.

Athletic Trainer (not currently offered)

Student athletic training gives students a hands-on opportunity to help fellow student athletes. Students with a background in anatomy can apply their knowledge to real-time athletic injuries and recovery, learning more about muscles, bones, ligaments, and treatment routines.

Badminton (offered in spring)

Webb badminton emphasizes hard work, fun, and team culture. Competition includes individual, doubles, and mixed doubles, and typically consists of 16 to 18 matches. Players must provide gym shoes.

Baseball (offered in spring)

Players learn fundamentals of fielding, hitting, and running while also developing more advanced concepts for the varsity level. Webb offers junior varsity and varsity levels designed to accommodate interested athletes of all abilities. Players must provide their own cleats and glove.

Baseball Clinic (not currently offered)

Baseball Clinic introduces participants to all aspects of the game, including fielding, hitting, pitching, and conditioning. One to two intra-squad games are planned each week.

Basketball (offered in winter)

Webb's basketball program emphasizes competitiveness, integrity, sportsmanship, and character development. Multiple levels of play are offered, and teams typically play 16 to 20 games. Players must provide gym shoes.

Basketball Clinic (offered in fall)

Basketball Clinic allows both new and experienced players to improve their skills during the off-season, with a focus on dribbling, ball handling, passing, shooting, and scrimmaging.

Board Sports (not currently offered)

Board Sports offers a Southern California experience through skateboarding and surfing. Students set goals at the start of the season and work toward them throughout, with several beach visits planned depending on ocean conditions and transportation availability.

Cross Country (offered in fall)

Cross country focuses on distance running, developing both cardiovascular fitness and mental fortitude. Competitions are 3-mile races, and both junior varsity and varsity teams are offered. Required equipment includes running shoes, lightweight clothing, and a water bottle.

Football (offered in fall)

Football emphasizes physical and mental skill, teamwork, and school tradition. The season consists of 10 games, and summer practice is encouraged for local families. Each player must provide cleats; all other equipment is furnished by Webb.

Functional Fitness (offered in fall, winter, and spring)

Functional Fitness helps students pursue fitness goals through strength and conditioning training. Students learn the basics of strength training and safe conduct in a weight room, and more advanced students may design programs tailored to their own goals. This activity is open to students of all fitness levels.

Golf (girls in fall, boys in spring)

The girls and boys golf teams provide athletes opportunities to grow their golf skills while bonding with teammates. Students should have a basic understanding of the game and some prior experience. Teams practice at Marshall Canyon. A full set of golf clubs is required.

Golf Clinic (not currently offered)

Golf Clinic provides off-season golf skill development and may include time at the driving range, putting green, golf course, and conditioning sessions with professional swing coaches.

Running Clinic (not currently offered)

Running Clinic is designed for students interested in distance running and possibly cross country or track and field. Students use the natural setting of the Webb campus and surrounding area for daily runs and should come prepared to improve cardiovascular fitness and embrace distance-running challenges.

Soccer (offered in winter)

Soccer practices and games focus on developing the technical, tactical, physical, and psychological demands of the game in a fun but challenging environment. Webb fields both junior varsity and varsity teams. Students need shin guards and cleats.

Softball (girls in spring)

The girls' softball team helps student athletes develop throwing, hitting, fielding, and running skills with an emphasis on teamwork. Players of all levels are encouraged to participate. Athletes need a softball glove and cleats.

Softball Clinic (not currently offered)

Softball Clinic introduces participants to the sport by covering fielding, hitting, pitching, and conditioning, with one to two intra-squad games planned each week.

Swimming and Diving (offered in spring)

Swimming and Diving is open to swimmers and divers of all abilities. Practices begin with technique and endurance work and progress to strength and speed. Home meets are held at the Barbara Mott McCarthy Aquatics Center. Swimmers need training suits, a towel, and goggles. Divers are encouraged to join whether beginning or experienced.

Tennis (girls in fall, boys in spring)

Tennis is a life-long sport that develops focus, patience, strength, and endurance. Students learn strategies for singles and doubles while building sportsmanship and control. Individual rackets and appropriate tennis shoes are required.

Tennis Clinic (offered in winter)

Tennis Clinic allows both new and experienced players to improve their skills during the off-season. Practices focus on skill development and include ladder-style tournaments.

Track and Field (offered in spring)

Track and Field includes a wide variety of events, from sprints and distance races to shot put, discus, long jump, triple jump, high jump, and pole vault. Meets typically occur once a week, with additional Saturday events. Athletes must provide event-appropriate footwear.

Triathlon (offered in winter)

The triathlon program introduces athletes to swimming, biking, and running. Students do not need to be experts in any area to participate. At least one off-campus race is typically required during the season. Students must provide running shoes and a bathing suit and have access to a bicycle.

Volleyball (girls in fall, boys in spring)

Webb's volleyball program focuses on setting, hitting, passing, and communication. Multiple levels of play are offered so that new and experienced players alike can find the program challenging and rewarding. Players must provide gym shoes and knee pads.

Water Polo (girls in winter, boys in fall)

Water polo combines elements of swimming, wrestling, soccer, and basketball. Webb offers junior varsity and varsity teams for athletes of all experience levels. Students must provide a swimsuit and goggles.

Wrestling (offered in winter)

The wrestling team focuses on character, integrity, and proper wrestling technique for athletes of all experience levels and backgrounds. Competition usually includes one meet per week and four weekend tournaments. Wrestlers must provide wrestling shoes and headgear.
